⚓   I. The Founding   ⚓
The Ledger Opens

You have just signed the founding papers of a merchant house. The ink is still wet on the second page. This is either significant or the notary was in a hurry; you are not yet certain which.

Verantia is built on reclaimed marsh, which explains its ambition and the smell. The council chamber leans slightly east. No one mentions this. The city has been performing respectability for three generations, which means it has had enough practice to almost succeed.

Three families have been here longer than your house, and they have not forgotten it. The Borracchi control the eastern quay and perform generosity the way weather performs calm — as a preparatory condition. The Spinetta lost the silk route in a bad contract five years ago and have since distributed blame across the city; none of the recipients are wrong. The Calmari appear at council, say nothing, and leave with what they came for. Their palazzo is modest. This does not account for their influence. No one has explained where the difference lives.

You are not yet a threat to any of them. This will change, or it will not. The ledger is watching either way.

⚔   II. The Year   ⚔
Two Demands, One Reckoning

A year in Verantia has a rhythm, and it does not negotiate.

The house speaks first. Your palazzo — the one with the roof that has always leaked — requires attention. The family name requires attention. The rival families require attention of a different kind. Decisions must be made from incomplete information, usually before the second letter of the morning. This is called governance. The Calmari call it something else.

When the house is done with you, the sea takes its turn. Your fleet — however many ships constitute your fleet at this particular moment — is running routes. Cargo spoils. Captains drink. The Caldera Straits collect their toll whether or not you are watching. Something will go wrong. The question is only which something, and whether you are positioned to survive it.

Then the year closes its books. You are older. Your heir is older. The ledger adds a page. Occasionally — rarely, when the ordinary routes have been exhausted and something stranger arrives — the year holds its breath and asks more of you than usual. These moments are not announced in advance. Neither were the ones that made the Borracchi what they are.

✦   III. The Ledger   ✦
Four Things That Can Be Lost

The ledger at the top of every page tracks four things, because these are the four things that end a house.

Treasury. You begin with eight hundred marks — enough to open a credit line at the harbour exchange, not enough to impress the Borracchi, which means you are precisely where a founding house should be. Watch the number. When it reaches zero, the house does not close quietly.

Name. In Verantia, reputation is not vanity — it is the rate of exchange between what you are and what people will permit you to do. A name in standing means contracts arrive before you ask, tariffs are quietly lower, and rivals return your correspondence. A name in decay means the reverse of each of these, progressively, until something worse that is not on any list.

Ships. You begin with one. A fleet of one is technically still a fleet. Lose it and you have nothing moving on the water, which in an archipelago is the same as having nothing moving at all.

Time. You are twenty-eight. The body keeps its own ledger and has never once accepted a renegotiation.

Allies. You are not alone. Casso commands your ships. Pell keeps the accounts. Tucci controls the harbour — or believes he does. They will speak when they have something to say. Listen when they do.

☠   IV. The Heir   ☠
What the House Becomes After You

You will die. This is not pessimism. It is the foundational accounting principle of the house.

When you do, the ledger passes to your heir. The ships, such as they are. The debts, precisely as large as you left them. The reputation, for better or considerably worse. The enemies — these come without annotation; the heir will have to discover them the same way you did. The palazzo with the leaking roof, which you will have either repaired by then or bequeathed as a structural metaphor.

Your heir has a character you did not shape and cannot revise. A reckless heir treats the treasury the way weather treats coastlines — with momentum and without apology. A cautious one counts twice and occasionally a third time and sometimes does not act until the moment has passed, which is its own kind of loss. A diplomatic heir says the right thing to the right person and something different but equally right to the next one, and both remain true, which is a skill that either builds empires or dissolves them.

When you die, the page turns. The house continues. What you built determines what they inherit. Build with this in mind, or do not — but the ledger will record which choice you made.

✦   V. Your Allies   ✦
Three Who Will Speak Truth

There is a man named Pell who keeps the warehouse accounts. He has been with the house since the founding — which is to say, since this morning — and he arrived with the inventory list and the expression of someone who has already identified three problems and is deciding in what order to mention them.

He has honest eyes. In Verantia, this is simultaneously a character virtue and a professional liability. He has arranged, somehow, for it to be both.

Casso is elsewhere — probably on a ship, probably arguing with a captain about something that matters. Tucci is at the harbour, controlling it or believing he does. The sea keeps its own counsel; the harbour keeps other people's.

In the corner of the screen, a small door: Consult the Archivist. Open it at any moment and Pell will read the current state of things — the treasury, the name, the year, what the sea has most recently done — and offer a single observation. Not counsel, exactly. An observation with an implication attached, the way a letter arrives already opened.

He is not always correct. He is always honest. In the context of Verantia, the distinction matters less than you might hope.

The ledger is open. The sea is waiting. The Borracchi are already smiling.

Pell — The Archivist
